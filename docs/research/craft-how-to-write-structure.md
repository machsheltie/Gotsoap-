# Craft research: how credible sources describe writing well and structuring story

## Executive synthesis

Across the sources, the strongest reusable craft rule is **causality plus reader work**: a story is not just events in order, but a chain of causes, consequences, reversals, and withheld implications that the reader actively completes. Robert McKee pushes that idea hard for commercial narrative, defining story through balance, inciting incident, desire, antagonism, and turning points. Jane Alison is the important counterweight: she argues that not every effective story has to imitate the same Aristotelian arc, and that spirals, meanders, radial structures, networks, and fractals can be equally purposeful when they match the material.

For prose, the durable advice is not “be beautiful” in the abstract but **control the technical channels that create reader experience**: sentence rhythm, syntax, punctuation, POV distance, and the degree of explanation. Ursula K. Le Guin treats craft as musical and physical—sentence structure, pauses, clarity, and punctuation are the working parts of prose. The Oregon State free-indirect discourse guide shows how point of view becomes craft, not just grammar: close third works when the narration absorbs the character’s worldview without announcing itself. George Saunders’s recent interview pushes the same reader-trust principle: don’t over-explain; assume the reader is intelligent and let the text earn their cooperation.

Revision is its own craft layer. John McPhee is the clearest source here: draft first, then revise multiple times, and treat revision as the place where writing really begins. That suggests a skill design that separates **structural diagnosis** from **line polish** and teaches users to decide which problem they are actually solving. Francine Prose’s craft book tradition supports the same kind of fine-grained diagnosis: details, dialogue, gestures, and paragraph-level choices are not decoration; they are how character, tension, and meaning get carried.

### Six to ten principles to encode in `writing-craft`

1. **Treat causality as the backbone of story.** If you can reorder the scenes without breaking the narrative, you probably have sequence, not plot.
2. **Design scenes/chapters as units of change.** They should turn, complicate, or answer a question; otherwise they are candidates for compression or deletion.
3. **Do not hard-code one arc shape as universal.** Arc, spiral, meander, radial, and network patterns are all valid when they fit the story’s pressure.
4. **Write for reader participation, not reader rescue.** Withholding, omission, subtext, and implication are features, not defects.
5. **Make prose technical before ornamental.** Rhythm, syntax, punctuation, and POV control create the felt quality of the prose.
6. **Hold POV stable and intentional.** Close third, free indirect style, and psychic distance are tools for controlling intimacy and irony.
7. **Let dialogue and detail do double duty.** Good dialogue advances plot and reveals motive; good detail selects rather than catalogs.
8. **Revise by layer.** First solve structure and causality, then sentence music, then micro-clarity and line-level texture.
9. **Prefer specificity over generic “good writing” advice.** Encode recognizable moves and diagnostics, not slogans.
10. **Treat omission as a deliberate craft decision.** Leave out what the reader can infer, but keep enough orientation to preserve coherence.

## Proposed `writing-craft` resource shape

### 1) `writing-craft/story-architecture.md`
**Purpose:** how story units work at scene, chapter, arc, and project scale.

**Should contain:**
- causality vs sequence
- scene/chapter as change units
- escalation that can be emotional, moral, informational, or relational
- setup/payoff tracking
- turning points, reversals, and forward pull
- alternative structures (spiral, meander, radial, network, fractal)
- diagnosis questions: “What changes?”, “What is the pressure?”, “What is the reader waiting for?”

### 2) `writing-craft/prose-craft.md`
**Purpose:** how to make prose carry feeling and meaning on the page.

**Should contain:**
- sentence rhythm and variation
- syntax and punctuation as effect systems
- specificity and sensory selection
- psychic distance / close-third control
- free indirect discourse
- detail selection
- dialogue that reveals motive through subtext
- omission and inference

### 3) `writing-craft/revision-craft.md`
**Purpose:** how to diagnose and fix draft problems.

**Should contain:**
- separate structural revision from line revision
- first draft vs subsequent drafts
- symptom-to-layer mapping: pacing, causality, voice, clarity, texture
- when a fix is a cut, reorder, rewrite, or line polish
- “what problem am I solving?” checkpoints
- draft workflow templates

### 4) `writing-craft/reader-contract.md`
**Purpose:** the practical reader-side contract that governs omission and explanation.

**Should contain:**
- trust the reader / don’t over-explain
- how much orientation is enough
- when to withhold versus when to clarify
- maintaining coherence while preserving uncertainty
- maintaining reader cooperation without flattening tension

### 5) `writing-craft/source-notes.md`
**Purpose:** a durable annotated index of craft references.

**Should contain:**
- source title, author, year, link
- what craft problem it speaks to
- notable claim or method
- confidence level (primary / near-primary / secondary)
- whether it generalizes across genres or is genre-specific

## Red flags to avoid encoding

- **“Three-act structure is the structure.”** Too rigid; it should be one option, not the default ontology.
- **“Show, don’t tell” as a blanket rule.** Some telling is efficient, necessary, and structurally right.
- **“Kill all adverbs / never use a dash / always write close third.”** Absolute bans are weaker than purpose-based decisions.
- **“Scene = conflict only.”** Scenes can be about revelation, selection, transition, or emotional repositioning.
- **“Explanation fixes all confusion.”** Over-explanation often destroys subtext and reader participation.
- **“Style is decoration after plot.”** The sources treat style as part of story delivery, not a final coat of paint.
- **“Revision means line editing only.”** Many failures are structural and need reordering, cutting, or reframing.
- **“One good technique fits all genres.”** Structure and style need to match the story’s intended pressure and promise.
- **“Specificity means more detail.”** The craft tradition prefers the right detail, not the most detail.
