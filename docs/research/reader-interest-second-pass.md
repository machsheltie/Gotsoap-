# Reader interest, immersion, and reward: second-pass evidence

Scope: external evidence on what makes fiction/books interesting, immersive, rewarding, or worth continuing, compared against `skills/writing-principles`.

## 1) Executive synthesis

### What should remain in `writing-principles`
- **Transportation**: keep it. The literature treats “mental entry into the story world” as a real, measurable reader experience, not just a metaphor.
- **Aesthetic pleasure**: keep it. Stylistic attention, foregrounding, and literariness are not ornamental extras; they can change emotional response and rereading value.
- **Social simulation**: keep it, but keep the claim modest. Fiction does support character-mind modeling, but the cognitive gains literature are small/mixed; the reward is better described as readerly modeling/interpretation than guaranteed empathy training.
- **Flow**: keep it, but define it more precisely as **challenge-skill fit + smooth processing**, not just “readable challenge.” The reading-flow literature explicitly treats flow as distinct from suspense, presence, identification, and cognitive mastery.

### What should change
- **Add an explicit curiosity / uncertainty / prediction layer.** The current four channels do not directly name the main engine of “I need to keep reading”: information gaps, uncertainty reduction, anticipation, and reward from learning what happens next.
- **Tighten “social simulation” language.** Avoid implying strong, broad real-world social-cognition gains; the evidence is real but small and inconsistent.
- **Tighten “flow” language.** Flow is not the same as suspense, immersion, or easy reading. It is a challenge-fit state with a separate measurement tradition.
- **Make the aesthetic channel more specific.** The evidence points less to generic “beautiful prose” and more to foregrounding/defamiliarization: language that makes the reader notice, reinterpret, and feel.

### What is missing
- **Curiosity / information gap / reward**: likely the biggest omission.
- **Suspense / uncertainty management / prediction**: a close second; related to curiosity but not identical.
- **Possibly meaning / appreciation**: I did not verify this deeply enough in this pass, but it is a plausible fifth lens for literary reward, especially for books that reward reflection more than page-turning.

## 3) Proposed edits to `writing-principles` at the concept/resource level

1. **Add a named curiosity channel or subchannel**
   - Working name: `curiosity`, `uncertainty`, or `prediction reward`.
   - Purpose: cover “what happens next?” pressure, information gaps, suspense, and the reward of closure.
   - Relationship to existing channels: partially overlaps flow, but is distinct enough to warrant explicit naming.

2. **Refine `flow`**
   - Reword conceptually as: “challenge-skill balance and smooth processing that make reading feel absorbing and easy to stay with.”
   - Keep it separate from suspense and transportation.

3. **Refine `social simulation`**
   - Reframe as “readerly modeling of characters as minds.”
   - Avoid implying strong causal claims like “fiction improves empathy” unless the skill is intentionally discussing weak/mixed effects.

4. **Refine `aesthetic`**
   - Emphasize foregrounding/defamiliarization, rhythm, pattern, and attention management.
   - Treat style as a mechanism of reward and interpretation, not decoration.

5. **Add a small caution note about fiction labels**
   - The important variable is often the simulation stance and narrative structure, not whether the text is explicitly labeled fiction.

## 4) Red flags / claims to avoid over-stating

- **“Fiction makes people more empathic”** — plausible, but the effect is small and replication is mixed.
- **“Flow just means easy reading”** — too blunt; the flow literature is about optimal challenge, not triviality.
- **“Suspense = surprise”** — too narrow; curiosity research points to uncertainty, novelty, conflict, and reward, not surprise alone.
- **“Aesthetic pleasure is just pretty prose”** — too generic; the evidence points to foregrounding, defamiliarization, and interpretive attention.
- **“The fiction label itself is what makes stories immersive”** — too strong; at least one controlled study found little difference between fiction and factual framing.
