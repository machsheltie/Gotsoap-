# Got Soap? — Campaign Intent (the standard every word is judged against)

**Source:** Stacey, 2026-07-17, organized by the arbiter. **Status:** canonical intent.
**Companions:** `voice-bible.md` (the register), `style-lock.md` (locked decisions), `promo.txt` +
`gotsoapdescription.txt` (the origin texts), the five posters, the Behance gallery.

`voice-bible.md` says *how* the words sound. **This says what the campaign is FOR** — the thesis,
the two audiences, the conversion mechanic, and the bar. When a reviewer asks "is this line
working?", it is asking against this document.

---

## 1. What this is now

A satirical hygiene PSA parodying **Got Milk? (90s)** — thirst-trap visuals, PSA sincerity,
fragrance-ad drama. Heavy satire and social commentary.

**The website IS the campaign now.** The five posters were the *initial* campaign; the site is the
thing. Copy is not decoration on top of the posters — it is the campaign's current surface.

**The bar is The Onion.** Straight-faced, committed, no wink. Not polite. Edgy. Unafraid to go there.

---

## 2. The thesis — what we're actually skewering

Not "men are gross." **The double standard, and the entitlement under it.**

The target: the man who wants a woman who is polished, manicured, youthful, beautiful, and
*clean* — while he himself looks like he hasn't bathed since **Duck Dynasty** premiered. The bushy
beard. The five-day funk worn as a **lifestyle**. And the serene conviction that any woman would
be **"lucky to have him."**

That gap — between what he demands and what he offers — is the joke, the commentary, and the
entire campaign. Every line should be traceable to it.

**The one real line (from the voice bible, restated because it's load-bearing):** punch at
**behavior, ad tropes, and the double standard** — never at a person's body, race, class, or
identity. The reformed-hero exit stays open: the joke is always *"you could be the poster,"* never
*"you're disgusting and that's the end of it."* That's what keeps it clever instead of cruel, and
clever is the flex.

---

## 3. Two audiences, two entirely different jobs

The campaign must land for **both**, for **different reasons**. A line that serves only one is
half a line. The best lines serve both at once.

### To her — recognition, and the mic
She has lived this. Every dating app. Every "you smell nice?" panic. **We have ALL been there.**
Her reward is the **laugh of recognition** — being seen, and handed the locker-room wall.
**She is the distribution channel.** The copy must be something she wants to *send*.

### To him — aspiration, and the call to action
This is the Got Milk? job. That campaign didn't shame kids; it made milk what the people you
admire drink. **Ours must make him want to take the damn shower** — not out of shame, out of
wanting what the clean man has.

He is not the villain of the piece; he is the **convert**. The exit is always open.

### The three share vectors (design copy for these)
1. **Woman → her friends:** the laugh. "This is every man on Hinge."
2. **Woman → a man she knows:** the hint, delivered with plausible deniability.
3. **Man → his friend:** the roast. *"Bro, the reason you're not getting a second date — have you
   smelled yourself lately? Got Soap? has a quiz. Take it."*

Vector 3 is the one most campaigns miss and the one that scales. If men can't use this on each
other, we've built a women-only in-joke and lost half the reach.

---

## 4. The conversion mechanic — sexy sells, and it's the strategy

Got Milk? used **celebrities kids looked up to** to sell the message. So: **what do men look up to?**

Men who are handsome — physically everything a lot of men wish they were — and who have **zero
trouble finding quality women.** The posters use those **celebrity archetypes** deliberately, to
sell *sexy*, and to weld one equation into place:

> **Clean = hot. Clean = the man who gets the woman. Clean is the price of admission.**

This is the aspiration lever, and it is the campaign's actual conversion engine for men. Shame
closes him down; **aspiration opens him up.** The posters do this visually. **The copy has to do it
verbally** — and reviewers should check that it does, on every surface where he's the reader.

**To be unambiguous:** this is *"sex sells"* as a century-old advertising principle — aspirational,
fragrance-ad register, exactly what a real luxury cologne campaign does. **It is not explicit
content and must never become it.** The heat is in the *implication and the confidence*, never the
anatomy. Smolder, don't leer.

---

## 5. The call to action

Got Milk? made kids drink milk. **Got Soap? makes him shower.** Every campaign surface should be
walkable back to: **take the damn shower** — and, on-site, **take the Sniff Test.**

The quiz is the conversion instrument and the share unit. A funny site that doesn't move him to
the quiz has failed the brief.

---

## 6. The voice bar (non-negotiable)

Sharp · witty · **punching up** · subversive · unapologetic · unafraid to go there. Heavy satire.
**Not polite.** If a line could run in a wellness brochure, it's dead. If Stacey wouldn't say it
out loud without apologizing, sharpen it until she would.

## 8. How to judge a line (the review criteria)

1. **Thesis** — does it trace to the double standard, or is it just "ew, smelly"?
2. **Audience** — does it know if it winks at *him*, *her*, or both? (Best: both.)
3. **Aspiration** — where he's the reader, does it sell *clean = hot*, or does it shame him shut?
4. **CTA** — does it move toward the shower / the quiz?
5. **Shareable** — does it survive one of the three vectors? Would she *send* it? Would he *forward* it?
6. **Onion-grade** — straight-faced and committed, or hedged and polite?
7. **Explains itself?** — a joke explained is a joke killed. Trust the reader to land it.
8. **AI tells** — does it read machine-written? (See `llm-writing`: filling structure without
   purpose, conclusions without evidence, defining by negation, conversational bleed.)
9. **The line** — punches at behavior/trope/double standard, never at body/race/class/identity;
   reformed-hero exit open.
10. **Cohesion** — does it sound like the same hand that made the posters, and like every other
    surface on the site?
